CREW RESOURCE MANAGEMENT (CRM) — STANDARD OPERATING PROCEDURE

Section 1.0 — Purpose
This document establishes Crew Resource Management principles for all flight operations, in accordance with FAA Advisory Circular AC 120-51E.

Section 2.0 — CRM Definition
CRM is the effective use of all available resources — human, hardware, and information — to achieve safe and efficient flight operations. CRM applies to all crew members including flight crew, cabin crew, dispatchers, and maintenance personnel.

Section 3.0 — Core CRM Principles
3.1 Communication
- Use standard phraseology and callouts
- Practice assertive communication: state concerns clearly and directly
- Use closed-loop communication: sender transmits, receiver acknowledges
- Brief and debrief every flight segment

3.2 Situational Awareness
- Maintain awareness of aircraft state, environment, and crew status
- Monitor flight instruments even when not flying
- Speak up immediately if something does not look or feel right
- Use the "Two Challenge Rule": if a concern is raised twice and not addressed, the other crew member has authority to take control

3.3 Decision Making
- Use structured decision-making models (e.g., FORDEC: Facts, Options, Risks, Decision, Execution, Check)
- Time-critical decisions: act decisively, communicate intent
- Non-time-critical decisions: gather information, discuss options, decide as a crew
- If in doubt, choose the most conservative option

3.4 Workload Management
- Distribute tasks appropriately between PF and PM
- Prioritize: Aviate, Navigate, Communicate
- Delay non-essential tasks during high-workload phases
- Request assistance when workload exceeds capacity

3.5 Leadership and Followership
- The Captain has final authority (FAR 91.3)
- First Officers are expected to monitor, support, and challenge when necessary
- Effective captains create an environment where crew members feel safe speaking up
- Gradient authority should be minimized — rank does not prevent error

Section 4.0 — Threat and Error Management (TEM)
4.1 Threats: External factors that increase operational complexity (weather, ATC, airport conditions)
4.2 Errors: Crew actions or inactions that lead to deviation from intentions
4.3 Undesired Aircraft States: Positions, speeds, or configurations that compromise safety

TEM Strategy:
1. Anticipate threats during pre-flight briefing
2. Recognize errors when they occur — call them out immediately
3. Manage undesired states with established procedures
4. Trap errors before they become consequential

Section 5.0 — Fatigue and Fitness
- Crew members must self-assess fitness before each duty
- Report fatigue to the Captain — do NOT fly if impaired
- Use controlled rest procedures when available during cruise
- Caffeine is a temporary aid, not a fatigue solution
- Review FAR Part 117 duty time limitations before accepting assignments

Section 6.0 — Documentation
All CRM-related events (communication breakdowns, authority issues, significant threats) should be documented through the Aviation Safety Action Program (ASAP) or Safety Management System (SMS).
