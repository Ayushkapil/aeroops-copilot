ENGINE FAILURE PROCEDURES — STANDARD OPERATING PROCEDURE

Section 1.0 — Scope
This procedure covers flight crew response to engine failure or malfunction during all phases of flight, in accordance with FAR 91.7 and AC 120-80A.

Section 2.0 — Engine Failure During Takeoff Roll (Below V1)
If an engine failure occurs before V1:
1. Retard both thrust levers to idle
2. Apply maximum braking
3. Deploy speed brakes/spoilers
4. Apply reverse thrust on operative engine(s)
5. If unable to stop on remaining runway, steer to avoid obstacles
WARNING: Do NOT attempt to continue the takeoff if engine failure occurs below V1.

Section 2.1 — Engine Failure After V1
If engine failure occurs at or above V1:
1. Continue the takeoff
2. Rotate at Vr, maintain V2 or above
3. Maintain runway heading to 400 feet AGL
4. Follow engine failure departure procedure
5. Do NOT retract landing gear until positive rate of climb confirmed
6. Retract flaps on schedule when safe altitude is reached
NOTE: V1 is the decision speed. Once past V1, the aircraft is committed to takeoff.

Section 3.0 — Engine Failure During Climb
1. Maintain aircraft control — fly the aircraft first
2. Maintain minimum safe airspeed (V2 or Venr as appropriate)
3. Level off if unable to maintain altitude
4. Declare MAYDAY to ATC
5. Execute appropriate engine failure checklist
6. Consider fuel dumping if overweight for landing

Section 4.0 — Engine Failure During Cruise
1. Maintain aircraft control
2. Select maximum continuous thrust on operative engine(s)
3. Begin drift-down to single-engine ceiling if unable to maintain altitude
4. Declare MAYDAY to ATC
5. Plan diversion to nearest suitable airport
6. Consider terrain clearance during drift-down
CAUTION: Drift-down altitude may be below minimum en route altitude (MEA). Ensure terrain clearance.

Section 5.0 — Engine Failure During Approach
1. If engine fails before FAF: execute missed approach and declare emergency
2. If engine fails after FAF: continue approach if stabilized, go around if not
3. Increase approach speed by 5-10 knots to account for asymmetric thrust
4. Be prepared for directional control issues during landing rollout
5. Request emergency equipment standing by

Section 6.0 — Single-Engine Landing
1. Brief the approach: expect directional control challenges on rollout
2. Fly a slightly wider pattern to avoid steep turns toward dead engine
3. Use full landing flaps if conditions permit
4. Plan for a longer rollout — reverse thrust on one engine only
5. Do NOT shut down operative engine until cleared of runway

Section 7.0 — Documentation
All engine failure events must be documented in the flight report and reported through the Safety Management System (SMS). Maintenance must be notified immediately.
